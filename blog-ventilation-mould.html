Why Proper Ventilation Prevents Mould

Mould is one of the most common problems I see in Irish homes. It's not just unsightly — it can affect your health and damage your property. The good news? Most mould problems are preventable with proper ventilation.

Understanding the Problem

Mould needs three things to grow:

  1. Moisture — the key factor
  2. Warmth — room temperature is enough
  3. A surface to grow on — walls, ceilings, furniture

In Ireland, we can't control the weather, but we can control moisture levels inside our homes. That's where ventilation comes in.

Where Does Indoor Moisture Come From?

You might be surprised how much moisture a typical household produces:

  • Cooking — up to 3 litres per day
  • Showering/bathing — about 1.5 litres per shower
  • Drying clothes indoors — 5+ litres per load
  • Breathing and perspiration — 1+ litre per person per day
  • Houseplants, aquariums, pets

A family of four can easily produce 10-15 litres of moisture daily. Without ventilation, that moisture has nowhere to go.

How Ventilation Helps

Ventilation replaces moist indoor air with drier outdoor air (yes, even Irish outdoor air is usually less humid than unventilated indoor air). This prevents condensation from forming on cold surfaces.

Practical Ventilation Tips

In the Bathroom
  • Always use the extractor fan during and after showering
  • Keep the fan running for at least 15 minutes after you finish
  • Open a window slightly if no fan is available
  • Keep the door closed to prevent moisture spreading
In the Kitchen
  • Use the cooker hood when cooking
  • Cover pots when boiling
  • Open a window when cooking generates steam
In Bedrooms
  • Open windows briefly each morning
  • Don't push beds tight against cold external walls
  • Keep bedroom doors open during the day for air circulation
Throughout the Home
  • Never block air vents — they're there for a reason
  • Avoid drying clothes indoors if possible
  • If you must dry indoors, use a room with an open window or a dehumidifier
  • Keep some background heating in unused rooms

When You Already Have Mould

Small areas of mould can be cleaned with a proper mould remover (not just bleach, which doesn't kill the roots). But cleaning is pointless if you don't fix the underlying moisture problem first.

Large areas of mould, or mould that keeps returning, usually indicate a more serious issue that needs professional assessment.

Improving Ventilation

If natural ventilation isn't enough, consider:

  • Installing or upgrading extractor fans
  • Adding trickle vents to windows
  • Using a dehumidifier in problem areas
  • Mechanical ventilation systems for severe cases
